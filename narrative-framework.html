The Cryptomath Narrative Framework

A practical way to judge crypto narratives before they become obvious.

Quick answer

The Narrative Framework scores a crypto narrative across seven dimensions. Problem pressure, timing, liquidity path, attention velocity, product proof, distribution surface, and asymmetry.

It is not a prediction machine. It is a way to avoid chasing late consensus while keeping early ideas honest.

A strong narrative is not just a story people repeat. It is a story with pressure behind it, capital moving toward it, and enough proof to survive first contact with reality.
The Cryptomath

The seven dimensions

Each dimension is scored from 0 to 5. The total gives a research signal, not a buy signal.

1

Problem pressure

The narrative must answer a real frustration, structural shift, or new demand. The stronger the pressure, the less the narrative needs pure hype.

0 to 5Need
2

Timing

A good idea can still be too early or too late. Timing checks whether the market, infrastructure, regulation, and user behavior are aligned now.

0 to 5Window
3

Liquidity path

Narratives need a path for capital to enter. Tokens, listings, incentives, yields, unlocks, and liquid venues all matter.

0 to 5Capital
4

Attention velocity

Attention is not just follower count. It is speed, repetition, memetic clarity, and how quickly independent groups start using the same language.

0 to 5Mindshare
5

Product proof

The best narratives still need evidence. Usage, revenue, retention, developer activity, user behavior, or credible prototypes reduce narrative risk.

0 to 5Reality
6

Distribution surface

A narrative travels faster when founders, exchanges, apps, creators, communities, funds, and users all have reasons to spread it.

0 to 5Spread
7

Asymmetry

The final question is simple. Is the upside still meaningful compared with how obvious, crowded, and priced-in the narrative already is?

0 to 5Edge

Scoring bands

The score should guide research priority, not replace judgment.

0-10

Noise

Too weak, too vague, or too dependent on pure promotion.

11-20

Watchlist

Interesting early signal, but still missing proof or timing.

21-28

Active

Strong enough to research deeply and track regularly.

29-35

Crowded

Powerful narrative, but often late. The key question becomes entry quality.

How to apply it

Use the same grid every time. That is what makes the framework useful.

StepQuestionWhat to look for
1What problem creates pressure?A real pain point, market inefficiency, regulatory shift, cultural shift, or user behavior change.
2Why now?New infrastructure, new liquidity, better UX, fresh incentives, or a catalyst that did not exist before.
3Where can capital enter?Liquid tokens, upcoming launches, public markets, points, yield, funds, or exchange attention.
4Who repeats the story?Builders, users, analysts, funds, exchanges, communities, and creators using similar language independently.
5What proves it is real?Usage, revenue, retention, developer traction, public data, or repeated behavior across more than one project.
6How far can it spread?Simple memes, clear categories, easy onboarding, strong communities, and incentives for many actors to talk about it.
7Is there still asymmetry?Compare upside with consensus, valuation, crowding, unlocks, reflexivity, and downside risk.

Common traps

Most mistakes come from confusing attention with signal.

Late consensus

When everyone can explain the narrative perfectly, the easy part of the trade may already be gone.

Fake usage

Incentives can create activity that disappears as soon as rewards stop. Usage needs context.

No liquidity path

A strong idea with no practical way for capital to enter can stay intellectually right and financially irrelevant.
